Each Friday, a single realistic documentation scenario is published here. Apply the four JRS review conditions yourself before revealing how an experienced reviewer reads the same record. The conversation continues on LinkedIn.

01
The Record

The record below was submitted by a department manager as part of the annual performance review cycle. It has not yet entered the official system. A reviewer is about to apply pre-finalization review.

Submitted Record Annual Performance Review

Employee demonstrates a consistently poor attitude toward supervisors and colleagues. Performance has been below expectations throughout the review period. Employee does not take initiative and has struggled to complete assignments on time. Communication style is frequently unprofessional.

The record reads coherently. Each sentence describes something. A reviewer who knew the employee, the manager, and the work history might find the document sufficient. But the next reviewer to read this file may be someone with none of that context.

Before continuing, consider what an experienced reviewer would flag in this record.

02
The Reviewer’s Read

The reviewer reads four sentences. Each describes something. None of them can be verified from the file.

Flag
JRS Condition 1 — Observable Support
“Employee demonstrates a consistently poor attitude toward supervisors and colleagues.”

“Consistently poor attitude” is a conclusion. It cannot be reconstructed without reference to specific interactions. What dates? What interactions? What was said or done, and by whom? A later reviewer cannot verify this sentence from any documentation in the file as submitted.

Flag
JRS Condition 2 — Evidentiary Traceability
“Performance has been below expectations throughout the review period.”

“Below expectations” is a comparative claim. The file must contain or reference the expectations against which performance is being measured. What are the stated expectations? Where are they documented? No performance plan, job description, or prior review establishing a baseline is referenced or on file.

Flag
JRS Condition 1 — Observable Support
“Employee does not take initiative and has struggled to complete assignments on time.”

“Does not take initiative” and “struggled to complete assignments on time” are both generalizations. A later reviewer cannot verify either without specific assignments named, dates of submission, and documented deadlines. How many assignments? Which ones? How late? None of this is in the record.

Flag
JRS Condition 1 — Observable Support
“Communication style is frequently unprofessional.”

“Frequently unprofessional” requires specific instances. What communications? On what dates? To whom? What made them unprofessional as documented at the time? A characterization of communication style is not the same as a record of communication.

JRS Condition Review — Pre-Finalization Assessment
Condition 1 — Observable Support
Fail
Every conclusion rests on a characterization. No specific incident, date, or interaction is named.
Condition 2 — Evidentiary Traceability
Fail
No record, document, or baseline is referenced or traceable from the file.
Condition 3 — Chronological Clarity
Fail
No timeline, date, or sequence is established for any event described.
Condition 4 — Reconstructability
Fail
A later reviewer, arriving without context, has nothing to reconstruct.
Result

Stop. Return to drafter. The record as submitted does not pass pre-finalization review on any condition. It may accurately reflect the drafter's assessment of the employee. It cannot be verified, defended, or reconstructed by anyone who was not present when it was written.

03
A Supported Revision

This is not the only way to write this record. It is one example of a revision that passes JRS pre-finalization review. The underlying facts are illustrative — a reviewer applying JRS would return the original to the drafter and ask them to supply the actual documented events from the file.

Revised Record — Passes Pre-Finalization Review Illustrative Example

On February 14, 2026, the employee received a documented verbal counseling regarding behavior toward a colleague during a team meeting; a written summary was signed by the employee and filed on February 17. On March 3, the employee was assigned a project deliverable due March 17. The deliverable was submitted on March 24 — seven business days past the deadline — without a prior extension request. On March 10, the employee sent an email to two department supervisors that was reviewed by HR and documented as requiring follow-up; a copy is retained in the file. No formal performance improvement plan was in place during the review period. The events listed above constitute the documented record for this review cycle.

The revised record is longer. It is less conclusory. It is also more difficult to write, because it requires the drafter to locate and reference specific documentation rather than summarize an impression.

Notice what the revision does not include: characterizations. It does not call the employee's attitude “poor” or their communication “unprofessional.” It describes what happened, with dates and references. A later reviewer arriving without context can read the file and understand what the record documents.

04
The Question

“When is the original version a problem, and when is it acceptable?”

Some organizations have informal norms about annual review language that have accumulated over time and are understood by everyone in the room when the review is being read. The drafter knows what the record means. The manager knows. HR knows. The question is what happens when that room changes — when the matter is elevated, when the personnel change, when the record is read a year later by someone who was not part of that organization's context.

The original record may accurately reflect reality. JRS does not evaluate whether the manager's assessment was correct. It evaluates whether the record, as written, can survive the moment when someone reads it without access to the knowledge the drafter had when writing it.

That is the question this edition puts to the LinkedIn discussion.
