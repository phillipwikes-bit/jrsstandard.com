Module 01The Five Pre-Finalization Review Questions
Not Started ► Open

Before any record enters an official system, apply five questions. These evaluate whether the supporting basis is present in the file — available to someone who was not involved in the original events. Review practices are often applied in different sequence depending on workflow structure.

1
Can this record stand on its own?A reviewer inheriting the file may have no access to the original author. The basis should be reflected in the underlying documentation without requiring additional explanation.
2
What specific evidence anchors each conclusion?Dates, logs, documented interactions, or referenced records. Verify referenced attachments are present in the file. Later reviewers may not know whether referenced materials were missing at drafting time or became unavailable afterward.
3
Is the path from evidence to conclusion visible in the record?Not in the author's recollection. Identifiable from the materials reviewed and documented in the file.
4
Are dates, timelines, and policies identifiable?Missing dates are among the most common single gaps. Pattern claims require specific dated instances.
5
Were characterizations verified against source material before finalization?Human reviewer should confirm. Source records should be present and identifiable before system entry. Where source records are partially available, note the limitation in the file.

The following examples show how these questions apply to ordinary administrative records — not only high-stakes escalations. Most documentation gaps occur in routine records that were never expected to face scrutiny.

Routine Example A — Scheduling documentation

Gap present
"Employee has ongoing scheduling conflicts that affect team operations."
▶ No dates. No description of which scheduling conflicts occurred or when. A later reviewer cannot reconstruct what the concern was or whether it was addressed.
Anchored
Employee arrived 35–40 minutes late to the Tuesday team standup on March 4, March 11, and March 18. Supervisor discussed attendance expectations on March 19. Employee acknowledged the conversation. Note on file.

Routine Example B — Accommodation follow-up

Gap present
"Employee's accommodation request has been reviewed and an interactive process was completed."
▶ No dates for the request or the interactive process. No record of what was discussed, what was offered, or what determination was reached. The basis for the conclusion is absent.
Anchored
Employee submitted accommodation request February 12. Interactive process meeting held February 19 with HR and employee's supervisor. Employee requested modified schedule; approved through March 31. Written confirmation provided February 20. All documents on file.
From practice

Records that do not hold up during later review often looked complete to the person who drafted them. The gap is generally not intentional — it reflects the difference between what the author understood at the time and what is actually documented in the file. Reviewer assumptions can carry forward into records over time, particularly where templates are reused from prior review cycles. Some records arrive with references to prior counseling discussions from an earlier supervisor where contemporaneous notes were not retained; the reference is present in the record but the underlying documentation is not. Certain supporting attachments from earlier review cycles may no longer be recoverable.

Module 02Identifying Common Documentation Failure Modes
Not Started ► Open

The following gaps appear across record types. Each is rarely obvious at drafting time but tends to surface when a later reviewer reads the file without prior knowledge. Unsupported wording often carries forward through copied templates or inherited summaries, particularly in organizations where escalation language is reused across review cycles without being verified against current source material.

Failure Mode 1: Evaluative Adjectives Without Behavioral Anchors

Before
"Employee has demonstrated unprofessional and hostile behavior toward colleagues."
▶ No specific conduct described. No dates. Nothing in the file to recover what occurred.
After
On April 4 and April 11, employee interrupted two client meetings after receiving written instruction on March 28 regarding meeting protocols (on file). Incident report dated April 12 covers both incidents.

Failure Mode 2: Pattern Claims Without Dates

Before
"Employee has been repeatedly absent without advance notice on multiple occasions."
▶ "Repeatedly" and "multiple occasions" — no dates. The pattern cannot be independently established.
After
Employee was absent without advance notice on January 5, January 12, and February 3, contrary to the Attendance Policy acknowledged January 2. Counseling note issued February 4 on file.

Failure Mode 3: Escalation Without a Prior Counseling Trail

Before
"After repeated coaching and progressive discipline, employee has failed to demonstrate improvement necessary to continue employment."
▶ "Repeated coaching" — no coaching notes referenced. "Progressive discipline" — no prior records identifiable in the file.
After
Employee placed on Performance Improvement Plan January 10. Check-in notes from January 24, February 14, March 3 reflect continued gaps. Written warning issued March 6. Employee acknowledged PIP and warning in writing. HR secondary review completed March 12. All records on file.

Failure Mode 4: Conclusions Surviving After Source Loss

Before
"As documented in prior incidents on file, employee has a pattern of conduct consistent with the current allegation."
▶ The referenced "prior incidents" are not identifiable in the current file. Records may survive after supporting attachments become inaccessible due to retention schedules or system migration.
After
Prior incidents referenced: incident report dated September 14 and counseling note dated October 2, both currently on file in the personnel record. If referenced records are unavailable, the pattern claim cannot be independently established and should not be relied upon in the escalation summary.

Routine Example — Ordinary counseling note

Before
"Counseling session held with employee regarding performance concerns. Employee was advised of expectations going forward."
▶ No date for the session. No description of which performance concerns. No record of what expectations were communicated. Cannot be reconstructed.
After
Counseling session held April 7. Manager reviewed three missed report deadlines (March 18, March 25, April 1). Employee confirmed awareness of reporting schedule. Next check-in scheduled April 21. Employee signed acknowledgment April 7. Copy on file.
Common pattern

Escalation files most often have problems at the timeline level. The conduct is described but the dates are not. Some records also reference prior counseling notes that are no longer available due to retention limitations or because records were migrated from an earlier system. Where the supporting records are not present in the file, the basis for the escalation conclusion may not hold up during later review. Historical escalation wording may carry forward through copied templates even where the original supporting context is no longer available. Some review environments also maintain separate supervisory and HR documentation, which can create gaps when records from different sources are combined into a single file.

Pause and Review

Before continuing, consider the records you commonly encounter. Some of the gaps below may also appear in records you have already reviewed without having been flagged at the time.

  • Can the conduct pattern be recovered from dates alone, without relying on the reviewer's recollection?
  • Are conclusions tied to records currently present in the file?
  • Would the record remain usable if the original drafter were no longer available — for example, following a staffing change?
  • Does the record reference materials that may not be accessible across retention cycles or system changes?

Module 03Secondary Review — When to Escalate and How
Not Started ► Open

Secondary review is typically triggered when a high-risk indicator is present in the record. Any single indicator is generally sufficient reason to route for secondary review before system entry. Review conclusions may differ where documentation is incomplete or inconsistently maintained.

Indicator
Evaluative language without a specific behavioral anchor
Indicator
Pattern conduct claim without specific dated instances
Indicator
Escalation without a documented prior counseling trail
Indicator
Termination without referenced supporting documentation
Indicator
AI-assisted wording not reviewed against source notes
Indicator
Accommodation record missing interactive process documentation

When an indicator is identified, complete the Secondary Review Escalation Form before the record advances. Secondary review may occur with incomplete supporting attachments or unresolved chronology gaps. In those cases, the gap should be noted rather than assumed resolved. Supporting materials may exist outside the primary personnel file depending on departmental workflow.

!
Stop — Return to DrafterEvaluative language without anchor. Missing timeline or policy basis. Do not submit until anchoring is present in the file.
~
Review RequiredBasis may be accurate but is not sufficiently visible. Clarification or additional anchoring needed before record entry.
✓
ReadySelf-contained. Supporting documentation present in file. Reasoning reflected in available materials. Basis holds without explanation from the drafter.
Reviewer note

Records that arrive without adequate supporting documentation are typically returned to the drafter rather than approved with a notation. In some departments, secondary review does not consistently occur for all record types. Wording that has appeared across multiple prior review cycles may not indicate that the underlying conduct was actually documented — reviewing the original source material, not just the language of the record, is how that distinction is made.

Module 04AI-Assisted Documentation Review
Not Started ► Open

Where AI tools contributed to a record, the review standard does not change. The review evaluates whether the basis is visible in the file regardless of how the wording was generated.

Generated Wording Reviewed Against Original Notes

Where AI-assisted drafting contributed to a record, the generated wording should be checked against the original notes before the record is submitted. Common patterns include: qualified or uncertain language in the source notes becomes a definitive statement in the generated record; general impressions appear as documented findings; disagreements or gaps in the notes are simplified or omitted. Earlier draft language may also carry forward through reused templates, introducing wording that was not verified against current source material.

Before review
"Employee has consistently demonstrated difficulty working within team structures and shown resistance to supervisory guidance."
▶ Source records not identified. "Consistently demonstrated" — no dates, nothing in the file to support this. "Resistance" — not attributed to documented interactions. Wording of this kind often carries forward from earlier drafts or inherited templates without having been verified.
After source review
Summary reviewed against meeting notes dated March 4, 11, and 18, and coaching records February 10 and March 3. On March 4, employee declined project planning participation without stated reason (meeting notes). On March 11, employee did not submit required status report despite two documented requests. Human review completed March 19. Confirmed against original notes. No unverified characterizations introduced.

AI content review sequence:

1
Source IdentificationList specific source records reviewed before drafting. Dates required.
2
Characterization ReviewEach characterization attributed to a specific documented interaction. Verify the generated wording does not go beyond what the notes support.
3
Conflict and Gap CheckConflicting accounts preserved. Gaps noted, not smoothed over. Generated summaries may compress conflicting details — check the original notes.
4
Human ConfirmationReviewer confirms wording against source notes. Confirmation documented before system entry. Certain departments may maintain supplemental tracking records for this step depending on local workflow.
5
System EntryRecord enters official system with human confirmation on file. Source records present and noted.
Reviewer check

If the source notes were removed, would the record still appear to have the same level of support? If yes, the basis may be in the wording but not in the underlying documentation. That is the gap worth examining before system entry.

Pause and Review

Before continuing, consider AI-assisted records you may encounter. Review routing for AI-assisted content may vary by department or staffing conditions.

  • Is the wording in the generated record supported by specific source interactions documented in the notes?
  • Does the generated record reflect disagreements or uncertainty that appeared in the original notes?
  • Has a human reviewer compared the generated wording against the original source material?
  • Could wording from an earlier draft have carried forward through a reused template without being checked against current materials?

Module 05Investigation Review and Conflicting Account Preservation
Not Started ► Open

Investigation records can be difficult to use during later review. The basis for the conclusion should be documented in the file so that someone reading it later can follow the reasoning without access to the original participants. Partial chronology is common where records came from multiple systems or where some earlier materials are no longer accessible. Some examples in this section are drawn from composite investigation scenarios.

Conflicting Account Preservation

Where accounts conflict, the conflict should remain documented in the file, including where one account is ultimately credited over another. Omitting conflicting accounts creates a documentation gap that tends to come up during later review. Conclusions may also remain in use after the original context is no longer easy to reconstruct. Documenting the basis for the finding — not just the conclusion itself — is what allows the record to stand independently.

Incomplete approach
"Based on the available evidence, the investigator found that the incident occurred as described by the complainant."
▶ Respondent account absent. Basis for crediting one account not stated. Conflicting evidence not acknowledged.
Supported approach
Complainant provided contemporaneous documentation dated April 3. Respondent denied the conduct and provided no supporting documentation. One witness corroborated complainant's account; one was not present. Credibility determination based on contemporaneous documentation and corroboration. Respondent's account noted and preserved.

Investigation review flow:

1
Complaint IntakeParties, allegations, and dates recorded before investigation opens.
2
Source CollectionWitnesses identified. Documents gathered. Each source logged. Note any materials that may be in archived systems not readily accessible.
3
Timeline ReconstructionEvents sequenced from documented sources. Gaps identified and noted. Later reviewers may encounter incomplete chronology where records were migrated or where earlier documentation is no longer available.
4
Conflicting Account PreservationAccounts that conflict with the finding remain visible. Not resolved away.
5
Finding ReviewFinding based on documented interviews, available records, and materials reviewed during the process. Reviewer assumptions not introduced as documented fact.
6
Final DocumentationRecord filed with sources identified, conflicting accounts preserved, and the basis for the finding documented within the record. Review completion may be documented separately depending on workflow.
Investigator note

The investigation record should be usable without the investigator available to explain it. Where following the record requires outside knowledge, the documentation may be incomplete. Staff changes, system migration, or retention schedules may mean that some earlier materials are not accessible during a later proceeding. Where reconstruction is partial, that limitation should be noted in the record.

Module 06Implementation — Starting and Expanding Review Practices
Not Started ► Open

No policy change, system integration, or formal rollout is required. Organizations often apply review practices unevenly across departments, and that is ordinary. Some review practices may already exist informally within existing supervisory processes. Certain review practices reflected in these materials were adapted from earlier supervisory review procedures retained during workflow consolidation.

1
Identify one record type to start withTermination records, accommodation documentation, or investigation conclusions are highest-leverage. Performance records are the most common entry point in practice.
2
Share the Redlined Examples with drafters and reviewersWork through the before-and-after examples. Identify which failure modes appear most commonly in your record types.
3
Apply the Pre-Finalization WorksheetDrafter or first-line reviewer applies the five questions before submission. Review sequencing may vary depending on staffing structure and record type. Reviewers may also rely on separate supervisory chronology notes not contained in the primary file.
4
Apply the Escalation Form when a high-risk indicator appearsAny single indicator is generally sufficient reason to route for secondary review before system entry.
5
Introduce the AI Verification ChecklistApply before any AI-assisted content enters an official system. Human confirmation documented before system entry. Unsupported wording from prior drafts may persist through copied templates — verify against source material each time.
6
Expand to additional record types as review practices stabilizeOnce established for one record type, introduce to other elevated-risk categories. Incremental. Uneven adoption across departments is common and expected during early rollout.
Implementation note

Most implementation gaps occur when review is added as a procedural requirement before reviewers find it useful in practice. Starting with one record type and one team tends to work better than a broader rollout. Reviewer inconsistency and partial adoption are ordinary and in some cases persist well into regular use. Historical records in inherited workflows may not meet current documentation standards.
