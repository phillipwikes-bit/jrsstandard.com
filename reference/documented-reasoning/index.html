Documented Reasoning

Short Explanation

Documented Reasoning is the FOLLOW element of the Minimum Viable Review Standard: how the reasoning moves from conduct to conclusion must be visible in the document. The reference is unambiguous: if the reviewer has to supply the reasoning, the record is not ready. A record can identify conduct, anchor it in time, and trace it to a source, and still fail if the connection from that basis to the stated conclusion is left to inference. Documented reasoning does not require length. It requires that the path from the documented facts to the outcome holds on the page, without verbal explanation from the drafter. When the reasoning is documented, a reviewer can test whether the conclusion follows. When it is absent, the reviewer can only observe that a conclusion was asserted.

Why It Matters

In escalation and proceedings, the question is whether the record shows the conclusion was reasonable, not merely that it was stated. Reasoning that lives only in the drafter's head cannot answer that question once the drafter is unavailable.

Reviewer Questions

  • Is it visible in the document how the reasoning moves from conduct to conclusion?
  • Does the reasoning hold without verbal explanation from the drafter?
  • Would the reviewer have to supply the reasoning to make the conclusion follow?
  • Are the applied standard and the basis for the assessment present in the record?

Common Failure Pattern

A record presents conduct and a conclusion with no connective reasoning. The reviewer must supply the logic, which means the record is not ready for submission.
