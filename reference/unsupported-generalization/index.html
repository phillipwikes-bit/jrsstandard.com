Unsupported Generalization

Short Explanation

Unsupported Generalization is a failure mode in which the language of a record claims more than its evidence supports. A single late submission becomes a pattern of unreliability. One control exception becomes a systemic weakness. The generalization may be small in wording and large in consequence, because the broadened claim is what a later reviewer reads and acts on. AI-assisted drafting raises the frequency of this failure: generative text tends toward fluent, confident, summarizing language that smooths specific, qualified facts into general statements. JRS treats the gap between the strength of the claim and the strength of the evidence as a reviewability defect. The remedy is not softer language for its own sake; it is language scoped to what the documented facts actually establish.

Why It Matters

Generalized claims are the easiest part of a record to challenge and the most damaging when they fail. A reviewer who finds that a broad characterization rests on a narrow fact discounts the entire record, including the parts that were sound.

Reviewer Questions

  • Is each claim scoped to what the documented evidence establishes?
  • Did a specific, qualified fact become a general statement in drafting?
  • Would the generalization survive if tested against the underlying record?
  • Has AI-assisted language strengthened a claim beyond its basis?

Common Failure Pattern

AI-assisted drafting converts qualified source notes into declarative findings. The finalized record overstates the facts, and the overstatement is the first thing a reviewer challenges.
