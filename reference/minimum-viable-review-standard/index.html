Minimum Viable Review Standard

Short Explanation

The Minimum Viable Review Standard is the floor to confirm before a record is finalized when full review is not possible. It is four elements, and if any one is missing the record is not ready. IDENTIFY: what conduct occurred, specific enough to describe without calling the author. ANCHOR: when it occurred, a date or traceable timeframe, not a general reference to a period. TRACE: what record, note, or source grounds the conclusion, identifiable in the file. FOLLOW: how the reasoning moves from conduct to conclusion, visible in the document, and if the reviewer has to supply it, the record is not ready. The standard is deliberately minimal so it can be met under real conditions: the goal is not perfect documentation but a record that can be reconstructed by someone who was not present.

Why It Matters

Aspirational standards are abandoned under time pressure. A concrete four-part floor gives reviewers and drafters a defensible threshold to meet before finalization and a consistent basis for what passes.

Reviewer Questions

  • IDENTIFY: is the conduct specific enough to describe without calling the author?
  • ANCHOR: is there a date or traceable timeframe, not a general reference to a period?
  • TRACE: is the grounding record, note, or source identifiable in the file?
  • FOLLOW: is the reasoning from conduct to conclusion visible without the reviewer supplying it?

Common Failure Pattern

A record is finalized missing one element, often the ANCHOR, so the pattern cannot be established and the file is not ready despite appearing complete.
