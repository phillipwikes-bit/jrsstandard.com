Observable Support

Short Explanation

Observable Support examines whether the conclusions in a record rest on documented, observable facts rather than on impressions, characterizations, or conclusions stated as if they were observations. It distinguishes what was seen, measured, recorded, or reported from what was inferred or felt. A record with observable support shows the underlying facts and lets the characterization rest on them. A record without it presents the characterization alone, so that a reviewer must take the author's judgment on faith. The standard is especially relevant where evaluative language is common, because evaluative terms can travel from a source note into a finalized record and harden into apparent fact. Observable Support asks the record to keep the observation and the judgment distinct and to show the observation.

Why It Matters

Characterizations that float free of observable facts cannot be tested and do not survive challenge. When an organization must demonstrate that a conclusion was grounded, only the observable basis carries weight; the characterization itself proves nothing.

Reviewer Questions

  • For each characterization, is the underlying observable fact documented?
  • Does the record separate what was observed from what was concluded?
  • Would the observable facts support the same conclusion if read by someone else?
  • Did evaluative language migrate from a draft into the record without its basis?

Common Failure Pattern

A record states an evaluative conclusion (the conduct was unprofessional, the control was inadequate) without the observable facts behind it. Under review the conclusion cannot be grounded and is treated as opinion.
