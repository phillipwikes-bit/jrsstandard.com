01
Practitioner Background

For twenty-seven years I worked inside the systems of public investigation, adjudication, and administrative review that produce the records organizations rely on when matters later need to be reconstructed.

The work spanned juvenile case management and community arbitration in Maryland's Department of Juvenile Services, followed by more than a decade at the Maryland Commission on Civil Rights, where I ultimately served as Lead Civil Rights Officer overseeing the Intake Unit. In that role I reviewed and approved investigative documentation produced by other Civil Rights Officers, across matters involving employment, housing, and public accommodations.

The Justification Review Standard, which I developed in early 2026, is the codification of practitioner judgment accumulated across those years. The same documentation failure patterns appeared again and again, regardless of matter type, regardless of drafter experience, regardless of whether the record was produced manually or with AI assistance. JRS is the working structure I wished had existed when I was the secondary reviewer reading other people's records and trying to decide whether they could survive later scrutiny.

05
Current Focus

The broader interest underlying the JRS framework is how organizational records are interpreted after the original context, participants, and surrounding circumstances are no longer available to the reviewer. This is the moment when documentation either holds or fails, and the work of pre-finalization review is to ensure that when that moment arrives, the record speaks for itself.

Current areas of focus include AI-assisted documentation review, documentation governance, evidentiary traceability, observable support for conclusions, reviewability of organizational records, and reconstruction-focused review methods.

The JRS Documentation Review Standard and the Investigator Field Guide are available without charge. Direct inquiries are welcome.
