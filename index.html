Justification Review Standard

A pre-finalization documentation review structure for organizational records. Applied before records enter official systems. Evaluates whether conclusions are understandable, supportable, and reconstructable from the file alone.

The review does not assess whether decisions were correct. It assesses whether the basis for decisions can be traced from the file. No software required. Works inside existing workflows. Can be introduced incrementally without structural change.

Review Standard

Documentation Review Structure

Records are reviewed later by people who were not present. The reviewer may have joined after the fact, inherited a file mid-process, or be reading the record months or years later. The file usually outlives the participants.

Review is applied at the drafting stage, before records enter formal systems. The review checks whether conclusions are understandable, supportable, and reconstructable from the file alone — not from the author's recollection, not from institutional memory, and not from context that was never written down.

Before Finalization, Ask

  • Can this record stand on its own?
    Would a later reviewer understand the basis without relying on institutional memory or additional explanation?
  • What specific evidence anchors this conclusion?
    A date, a documented interaction, a log entry, a referenced record. General impressions are not evidence.
  • Is the path from evidence to conclusion visible in the record?
    The review evaluates whether the basis remains visible in the document itself rather than depending on the drafter's recollection.
  • Are dates, interactions, and policies identifiable?
    Missing dates become critical during escalation. Most review problems start earlier than people think.
  • Would a later reviewer follow the same reasoning?
    Without access to context that exists only in the author's knowledge.

    • Four Review Conditions

    01
    Independently Reviewable
    Time Traveler Test
    The basis for conclusions should be recoverable from the record by someone with no prior knowledge of the events, without additional context or supplementary explanation. Records that depend on who is still around to explain them do not hold up over time.
    02
    Observable Support
    Fact Anchor
    Each conclusion requires a specific, verifiable anchor in the file: a date, a documented interaction, a log entry, or a referenced record. Without one, the conclusion cannot be independently verified. General impressions are not evidence.
    03
    Documented Reasoning
    Reasoning Trail
    The path from evidence to conclusion should be visible within the record. Conclusions that depend on unstated assumptions or context not present in the file do not satisfy this condition. A smooth narrative is not the same as a supported record.
    04
    Source Integrity
    AI Attestation
    Where automated drafting materially contributes to a record, the source material supporting substantive conclusions should be identifiable and reviewed by a human before finalization. The wording may sound accurate. That is not the same as being supported.
    From practice

    Secondary review most often flags evaluative adjectives without behavioral anchors, escalation conclusions without a prior counseling trail, and AI-assisted summaries without source identification. Pattern claims missing dates are the most common single gap.

    Operational Constraints

    Application may involve incomplete records, conflicting accounts, time pressures, or qualitative information requiring reviewer judgment. These conditions are acknowledged without eliminating the requirement for documented support.

    Documentation is sometimes written after events, from memory, under time pressure, or by reviewers with partial information. Depth of review will vary by workflow and staffing conditions. The four conditions do not vary.

    Implementation note

    Secondary review does not repair unsupported drafting. Records that arrive without a traceable basis should be returned to the drafter. Approving them with a notation that support is missing does not resolve the underlying problem — it enters the system unsupported and the notation does not appear in the final record.

    From practice

    Records with specific dates, referenced policies, and identified conduct hold up. Those that depend on unwritten context do not. The difference is rarely obvious at the time of drafting.

    Reviewer Environments

    Review Responsibilities by Function

    These environments were developed at different points and for different operational reasons. The depth varies by section. Where a section is thinner, additions will appear in subsequent revisions as implementation experience accumulates.

    Environment 01

    HR Reviewer

    HR reviewers apply the review standard to performance, disciplinary, termination, and accommodation records before those records enter official systems. Secondary review is the primary organizational control point for elevated-risk records. In practice, secondary review does not always occur before records enter official systems. That gap is what this environment addresses.

    Some organizations apply this review alongside legacy HR documentation systems that do not support pre-submission controls. In those environments, the review is applied manually at the drafting stage before the record is transferred into the official system.

    From practice

    Most HR secondary review returns happen at intake. The most common triggers are evaluative language without behavioral anchors and escalation conclusions without an attached prior counseling trail. Both are typically identified within the first two minutes of review.

    What HR Reviewers Are Reviewing For

    • Whether each conclusion has a specific, verifiable anchor in the file — a date, a documented interaction, a referenced record
    • Whether prior counseling, coaching, or warning records referenced in the record are actually attached and on file
    • Whether evaluative language is grounded in specific, dated behavioral descriptions rather than general characterizations
    • Whether AI-assisted wording, if present, has been reviewed against source notes and attested by a human reviewer before submission
    • Whether the record would be reconstructable by a later reviewer with no knowledge of the events
    Implementation note

    The depth of HR secondary review varies by workflow, staffing, and record type. Not every record receives the same level of review. The indicators below identify where secondary review is required regardless of workload conditions.

    Common Failure Patterns — HR Context

    Performance Records

    Performance evaluations frequently contain evaluative conclusions without a single specific dated instance of the described conduct, without reference to a documented performance standard, and without coaching records that would show the employee was aware of the gap. A record that reads as a complete performance assessment may contain no single anchored fact.

    From practice

    Well-written performance records are sometimes the least supportable. The language is professional. The basis is not in the file.

    Disciplinary Records

    Disciplinary conclusions frequently reference "repeated coaching" or "multiple warnings" without identifying the dates, participants, or file locations of those discussions. The escalation language is present. The counseling trail it presupposes is not in the file. This failure mode is most common when documentation is created at the point of formal action rather than throughout the underlying process.

    Termination Records

    Termination records are the highest-risk documentation type. Records that reference "ongoing performance issues" or "failure to improve" without a referenced performance plan, specific benchmark failures, and a documented counseling trail cannot support reconstruction by a later reviewer. Secondary HR review plus legal consultation is required before system entry.

    Accommodation Records

    Accommodation records frequently omit interactive process documentation: meeting dates, alternatives considered, employee responses, and the determination rationale. A denial noted as "operational needs" without specifying the constraint does not hold up under later review.

    Field observation

    Departments with the most documentation volume may still produce weak reconstruction records. Frequency of documentation does not correlate with evidentiary support within individual records.

    Escalation Indicators

    Any single indicator below is sufficient reason to stop before system entry.

    • Termination record without referenced and attached supporting documentation — hold, route to legal
    • Accommodation denial without documented interactive process steps — legal review recommended
    • AI-generated summary without source identification or attestation — return for source verification
    • Pattern conduct claims without specific dated instances — return to drafter
    • Evaluative conclusions without behavioral anchors — return before system entry
    AJL · 5/9/26

    No PIP referenced. Three counseling notes mentioned in narrative; none attached. Pattern claim unsupported. Timeline between January 15 and March 3 unclear. Returned to drafter before system entry.

    From practice

    Accommodation records are among the highest-risk documentation type for reconstruction failure. The interactive process steps, alternatives considered, and determination rationale must be in the file. Not reconstructed from the reviewer's recollection of the process.

    Environment 02

    Investigator

    Investigator review applies the review standard to witness summaries, incident records, and investigation conclusions before those records are filed. Investigation records are among the most likely to be examined under adversarial conditions. Investigator turnover makes reconstruction from the file — rather than from the investigator's memory — a practical necessity, not an edge case.

    From practice

    The investigation record must stand without the investigator available to explain it. If following the reasoning requires knowing what the investigator knew at the time, the record is not complete.

    Source Identification

    Source materials reviewed during the investigation should be specifically identified in the record. "A thorough investigation was conducted" describes the process. It does not identify the basis.

    • Interviews conducted: names, dates, and roles of witnesses
    • Documents reviewed: correspondence, logs, records, policy references
    • Prior records considered: performance history, prior complaints, relevant HRIS data
    Implementation note

    Source identification requirements vary by investigation type and organizational context. The underlying standard does not change: a later reviewer must be able to identify what was reviewed without contacting the investigator.

    Conflicting Account Preservation

    Where accounts conflict, the conflict must remain visible in the record. A later reviewer needs to see what was contested, not only what was concluded. Resolving conflicts through omission creates a gap that surfaces under adversarial review.

    This is the section of investigator review that most consistently requires attention. Investigation summaries tend to move toward narrative coherence. The source material often contains more ambiguity than the filed record reflects.

    From practice

    Investigation conclusions that omit conflicting accounts do not satisfy the Documented Reasoning condition. A later reviewer needs to see what was contested, not only what was concluded.

    Escalation Indicators

    • Conclusion used as basis for formal discipline — independent reconstruction review before action is taken
    • Source materials not specifically identified — return for identification before filing
    • Conflicting accounts not acknowledged in the record — return for revision
    • AI-assisted wording in investigation summary without attestation — hold pending source review
    Environment 03

    Compliance Reviewer

    Compliance reviewers apply the review standard in two primary contexts: pre-submission review of compliance documentation before it enters official systems, and periodic audit sampling of existing records for documentation quality.

    This environment was developed later than the HR and investigator environments and reflects a narrower range of implementation experience. Additions will appear as audit sampling findings accumulate.
    From practice

    Compliance audit sampling surfaces patterns that pre-submission review prevents. The two are complementary, not substitutes. Sampling identifies where the system is failing. Pre-submission review is where that failure is corrected.

    Common Failure Patterns — Compliance Context

    Policy Reference Gaps

    Compliance records frequently cite "company policy" or "applicable policy" without identifying the specific policy name, section, or version. A later reviewer cannot verify which standard was applied, whether the employee was aware of it, or whether the cited policy was in effect at the time of the conduct.

    Acknowledgment Gaps

    Policy-based conclusions require that employee acknowledgment of the applicable policy is documented and on file. Records stating that "employee was informed of expectations" without identifying when, how, or in what form do not satisfy this condition.

    From practice

    Policy-based conclusions without a traceable acknowledgment are the most common compliance documentation gap in audit sampling. The policy exists. The employee's awareness of it at the relevant time is not in the file.

    Review Examples

    Reconstruction Failure Examples

    The following examples show documentation failures as they commonly appear during later review. Each is drawn from a routine operational context. None are dramatic edge cases. The most significant reconstruction failures typically involve ordinary documentation gaps that were invisible at drafting and only became consequential when the file was reviewed without the original participants.

    From practice

    Most files that arrive for secondary review are not entirely empty. They contain something. A date, a reference, a general description. The question is whether what is there is sufficient to reconstruct the basis without the author. Usually it is not.

    RF·01 Missing Attachment — Counseling Note Referenced, Not Filed
    Record as filed
    "Employee was counseled regarding attendance on February 4. Counseling note on file."
    Reconstruction questions
    • Where is the February 4 counseling note? It is referenced but not attached.
    • What was communicated during the counseling session?
    • Did the employee acknowledge receipt? Is that documented?
    • Without the note, what does "on file" refer to?
    Disposition
    STOP. Referenced record not present. Record cannot be submitted until the February 4 counseling note is located and attached. If the note cannot be located, the gap must be documented explicitly. The reference alone does not satisfy Observable Support.
    Supported revision
    Employee was counseled regarding attendance on February 4. Counseling note dated February 4 attached — Tab A. Employee acknowledged receipt in writing February 5; acknowledgment attached — Tab B.
    The original record referenced a document that was not there. This is the most common attachment failure in secondary review. The reference creates the appearance of support. The absence of the document means the support is not in the file.
    RF·02 Chronology Gap — Escalation Without Visible Prior Steps
    Record as filed
    "Following repeated performance discussions and progressive documentation, employee is being placed on a formal performance improvement plan effective March 15."
    Reconstruction questions
    • What performance discussions occurred, and on what dates?
    • What does "progressive documentation" refer to? Which records?
    • Are those records attached or identifiable in the file?
    Disposition
    REVIEW REQUIRED. Escalation language present. Supporting trail not identifiable from the file. Return to drafter for attachment of prior documentation or explicit acknowledgment of what is and is not on file.
    Supported revision
    Employee placed on formal Performance Improvement Plan effective March 15. Prior process: verbal counseling February 1 (note on file — Tab A); written counseling February 22 (note on file — Tab B); performance check-in March 3 reflecting continued gap (notes on file — Tab C). PIP establishes specific benchmarks for the 60-day review period — attached Tab D.
    The original record read as the conclusion of a documented process. The file contained no evidence of that process. The manager had conducted the prior steps. They were not reflected in the file that reached secondary review.
    RF·03 AI-Generated Characterization — Source Notes Not Identified
    Record as filed
    "AI Summary: Employee has demonstrated a persistent pattern of resistance to management direction and has consistently underperformed relative to team expectations."
    Reconstruction questions
    • What source records were reviewed before this summary was generated?
    • What specific interactions support "persistent pattern of resistance"? Are those identifiable?
    • Has a human reviewer confirmed this summary against the source notes?
    Disposition
    STOP. Source records not identified. Human confirmation not documented. AI-generated characterizations not traceable to specific documented interactions. Do not submit.
    Supported revision
    Summary reviewed against meeting notes dated March 4, March 18, and April 1, and coaching records from February 28 and March 25. On March 4, employee did not complete assigned pre-meeting preparation (meeting notes — on file). On March 18, employee missed a submission deadline despite documented reminder March 15 (email on file). No unverified characterizations introduced. Human review completed April 3.
    The manager's notes described two missed tasks. The generated record described an ongoing pattern of resistance. Those are not the same characterization. The notes did not change. The wording did. A reviewer reading the submitted record had no way to know what the underlying notes actually said.
    RF·04 Unsupported Pattern Claim — Conduct Described, Dates Absent
    Record as filed
    "Employee has repeatedly failed to complete assignments on time, despite multiple reminders from the supervisor."
    Reconstruction questions
    • Which assignments were not completed on time? What were the deadlines?
    • What dates did the failures occur?
    • What reminders were issued, and when? Are they documented?
    Disposition
    STOP. Pattern conduct claim without dated instances. Reminder references without dates or documentation. Record does not satisfy Observable Support. Return to drafter.
    Supported revision
    Employee missed assignment deadlines on January 20 (project brief, due January 18), February 14 (status update, due February 12), and March 3 (final deliverable, due March 1). Supervisor issued written reminders January 19, February 11, and February 28; emails on file.
    The original record described a pattern. Without dates, a later reviewer cannot establish the pattern independently. This is the most common single documentation gap across all record types. The date is usually simply missing — not omitted intentionally.
    RF·07 Policy Referenced, Acknowledgment Absent — partial reconstruction
    Record as filed
    "Employee's conduct on April 14 violated Remote Work Policy Section 3.2. Employee was informed of expectations at the time of hire."
    Reconstruction questions
    • Which version of Remote Work Policy Section 3.2 was in effect on April 14?
    • Is the policy acknowledgment on file? The record states the employee was informed at hire — is that documented?
    • Without a traceable acknowledgment, how does a later reviewer confirm the employee was aware of the specific policy provision?
    Disposition
    REVIEW REQUIRED. Policy section identified — that portion is traceable. Employee acknowledgment not on file or referenced with sufficient specificity. "Informed at time of hire" is not independently verifiable from the file as filed. Return to drafter to identify and attach the relevant acknowledgment or document the gap explicitly.
    Supported revision
    Employee's conduct on April 14 violated Remote Work Policy Section 3.2 (remote work notification requirements), version effective January 1. Policy acknowledged by employee January 10 during onboarding; signed acknowledgment on file — Tab A. Employee was reminded of the notification requirement in the February 15 team meeting; meeting notes on file — Tab B.
    The original record correctly identified the policy section. The acknowledgment gap — the employee's documented awareness of the specific provision — was not in the file. Partial traceability is not full traceability. The policy can be found. Whether the employee knew it at the relevant time cannot be established from what was filed.
    RF·10 Witness Account Present, Corroboration Absent — partial reconstruction
    Record as filed
    "Complainant provided a written account of the March 18 incident. Respondent denied the conduct. Investigation found the complaint substantiated based on the complainant's account and available evidence."
    Reconstruction questions
    • What is "available evidence"? Is it identified in the record?
    • Were there other witnesses? What did they say?
    • On what basis was the complainant's account credited over the respondent's denial?
    Disposition
    REVIEW REQUIRED. Complainant's written account is present and identified. Respondent denial acknowledged. "Available evidence" not identified. Credibility basis not stated. Return for identification of the evidence reviewed and the basis for crediting the complainant's account.
    Supported revision
    Complainant provided written account of March 18 incident, dated March 18 — on file. Respondent denied the conduct during April 5 interview; interview notes on file. One colleague corroborated complainant's account of the interaction. No documentary evidence directly corroborating either account identified. Credibility determination: complainant's contemporaneous written account, prepared the same day, given greater weight than respondent's denial provided two weeks later without supporting documentation. Conflict noted and preserved.
    This is a partial reconstruction scenario: the evidence exists but the reasoning is not visible in the original record. The revision does not add evidence. It makes the existing evidence and the reasoning from that evidence visible.
    Later Review

    Later Reviewer Conditions

    These conditions describe the circumstances under which organizational records are most commonly reviewed outside their original context. They are not edge cases. They are routine features of HR, compliance, investigation, and administrative review environments.

    From practice

    Reviewers regularly inherit records written by people who are no longer with the organization, for decisions made months or years earlier, in contexts that no longer exist. The file is what they have.

    LC·01 Original Reviewer Unavailable

    The most common later-review condition. Personnel change, department reorganization, and ordinary turnover mean that the person who drafted or reviewed a record is frequently unavailable when the record is examined later. The file must stand without them.

    Review implicationRecords that depend on the drafter's recollection to supply context that is not in the file do not survive this condition. Records with specific dates, referenced policies, and identified conduct tend to hold.
    LC·02 Source Attachments Missing or Archived Separately

    Records frequently reference source documents — counseling notes, performance plans, incident reports, correspondence — that are not attached at the time of later review. A reference to a document that is not in the file does not satisfy Observable Support. The reference creates the appearance of support. The absence of the document means the support is not available for later reconstruction.

    Review implicationSecondary review confirms that referenced records are actually attached before the record enters the official system. A reference to "counseling note on file" without the note being attached is one of the most common secondary review triggers.
    LC·03 Incomplete Chronology — Timeline Cannot Be Closed

    Later review frequently reveals that a chronology described in a record cannot be independently verified from the file. Dates are missing. The sequence of events relies on the author's recollection rather than documented anchors. The pattern claim describes a progression that the file does not support.

    Review implicationMost escalation files fail at the timeline level. Conduct is described. Dates are not. Without specific dates, the sequence cannot be established independently.
    LC·04 Policy Version Unclear

    Records citing policy must identify the specific policy and section. Where the policy has been revised, the record should reflect the version in effect at the time of the conduct. Later reviewers cannot assume which version applied without a specific citation. This condition is most common in compliance documentation reviewed during regulatory audit, where the applicable standard at a specific point in time is at issue.

    Review implication"Company policy" is not identifiable. "Attendance Policy Section 2.1, acknowledged January 2" is.
    LC·05 Unresolved Conflicting Accounts in the File

    Where the file contains conflicting accounts and the conflict was not acknowledged or addressed, the later reviewer inherits an unresolved evidentiary problem. Investigation records are most vulnerable to this condition. Summaries that present a unified narrative where the source material contained disputes create a gap that is only visible when the original source notes are examined.

    Review implicationConflicting accounts should be preserved in the record, not resolved through omission. A later reviewer who discovers a conflict the record does not acknowledge will question the reliability of the conclusions that follow from it.
    LC·07 AI-Assisted Content Not Identified as Such

    Where AI tools contributed to a record and that contribution is not identified, a later reviewer reading the file has no way to distinguish AI-generated characterizations from the drafter's documented observations. Where an AI-assisted record reaches later review and the source notes are unavailable, the characterizations in that record cannot be verified.

    Review implicationHuman attestation before system entry is the control that makes AI-assisted content reviewable later. Without it, the later reviewer has no path from the conclusion to the documented source material.

    Legacy System Coexistence

    Organizations implementing pre-finalization review do not typically begin with a clean documentation environment. Existing records in official systems may not contain source-path visibility. Historical records may reference documents that no longer exist or were never attached. Prior documentation practices may not have required the same level of evidentiary traceability now applied to new records.

    The review standard applies to records entering official systems going forward. Where historical records are referenced in current documentation — as part of an escalation trail, disciplinary history, or prior conduct record — the reviewer notes any reconstruction limitations presented by those records rather than treating the historical record as satisfying current conditions.

    Implementation note

    Review depth in the first months of implementation is typically uneven. Some record types receive full secondary review. Others receive self-review only. That unevenness is normal. The standard does not change by record type or implementation stage.

    Institutional Conditions

    How Records Age

    The following describes conditions that arise not from individual documentation failure, but from the way institutions operate over time — layering new processes on unresolved old ones, losing interpretive continuity through personnel change, and accumulating practices whose origins are no longer recoverable.

    This section is more developed in some areas than others. Additions will appear as implementation experience accumulates.

    From practice

    Standards evolve. Records do not. A later reviewer reading an older record is always reading it against a standard that may have changed since the record was created. That gap does not resolve itself.

    Resolution Drift

    Resolution drift describes deferred conditions that aged into accepted file status without being resolved. The follow-up was assigned. The condition was documented. The file proceeded. At some point between the deferred disposition and the later review, the open condition stopped being tracked and became part of the background of the file.

    The gap didn't grow. It just never closed. The later reviewer finds the deferred notation and looks for the resolution. There isn't one.

    From practice

    The gap was documented. The resolution was not. Those are not the same condition, though the file may make them look identical by the time someone reads it.

    Implementation observation

    Deferred conditions closed by follow-up confirmation are only as reliable as the confirmation process. Where the confirming reviewer does not verify that the document provided matches the document requested, the deferred condition may be formally closed while remaining substantively open.

    Operational Amnesia

    Operational amnesia describes the condition in which an organization rediscovers a problem it previously identified, documented, and addressed. The corrective action was genuine. The documentation of the corrective action exists somewhere in the institutional record. Nobody currently responsible for the function knows about the prior finding or the prior response.

    The finding is not new. The organization's awareness of it is.

    Implementation observation

    Corrective actions that are not documented in a way that survives personnel change are effectively temporary. The fix operates as long as the people who know why it exists remain. When they leave, the institutional knowledge that sustained the fix leaves with them.

    Institutional Workarounds

    Institutional workarounds are informal operational practices that develop when the formal process is too slow, too unclear, too burdensome, or too disconnected from operational reality to be followed as written under actual workload conditions. They are not evasions. They are adaptations.

    The reconstruction problem they create is not that the outcome is wrong. It is that the record reflects the formal process, not the informal one. A later reviewer reading the record cannot see that a different process actually governed.

    From practice

    The record said secondary review was completed. Secondary review had been completed — informally, by phone, two days before the formal submission. The formal submission went through as a matter of record. The review that mattered had already happened. Its informal stage is not visible.

    Procedural Fossilization

    Procedural fossilization describes the condition in which a review step, form field, notation system, or process artifact continues to exist and be used after the process that required it no longer exists. The artifact is maintained by organizational inertia rather than by operational purpose.

    Example · ERP Code Field
    Artifact

    Performance evaluation forms include a field: "ERP Code." The field accepts a four-digit numeric entry. Approximately 60% of submitted evaluations include an ERP code. 40% leave it blank.

    Known origin

    The organization used an enterprise resource planning system until 2021. The ERP system was decommissioned and replaced. The ERP code field was not removed from the evaluation form during the transition.

    Effect on reconstruction

    A later reviewer examining a performance evaluation with an ERP code entered cannot determine whether the code refers to anything currently accessible. The ERP system has been decommissioned. No lookup for prior ERP codes is available.

    Legacy Vocabulary Persistence

    Legacy vocabulary persistence describes the condition in which terms from prior frameworks, policies, or systems continue to appear in organizational records after the frameworks that defined them have been retired.

    "Coaching Session"
    Prior meaning (pre-2020 framework)

    A defined formal step in the progressive discipline sequence: documented, dated, filed in the HRIS, and referenced in subsequent disciplinary records. Step one of a four-step process.

    Current use

    Used informally by managers and HR staff to describe any developmental or performance conversation, including conversations that have no formal documentation requirement under the current framework.

    Reconstruction gap

    A later reviewer cannot determine whether a "coaching session" reference indicates a formal documented step or an informal conversation. Whether it can be used to establish a progressive discipline trail depends on which framework applied when it occurred — a determination the record does not enable.

    Archive Fragmentation

    Archive fragmentation describes the condition in which documents that are relevant to the interpretation or reconstruction of current records are distributed across repositories that secondary reviewers and later reviewers do not routinely access, do not know to access, or cannot access without a separate request process.

    The documents exist. They are retained. They are not hidden. They are simply in a location that is not part of the standard review path. The record does not tell the later reviewer where to look.

    From practice

    The answer to the interpretive question was in a memo that had been in the compliance archive for three years. Nobody reading the records produced under that interpretation had ever seen the memo. The question kept getting re-asked.

    Later observation

    The archive contains the institutional memory that would make ambiguous records interpretable. The archive is not accessible through the standard review path. The records do not reference the archive. The institutional memory and the records it would clarify exist in parallel, without connection.

    Operational Notes

    Operational Note · May 2026
    The review conditions apply regardless of how a record was drafted. AI-assisted records are evaluated against the same four conditions as manually drafted records. The source of the wording does not change the standard for identifiable support.
    Operational Note · May 2026
    Secondary review is listed as required for termination, accommodation, formal discipline, and investigation records. In practice, what this means operationally depends on staffing, workflow structure, and organizational size. The condition does not change. How it is met varies.
    Field Observation · May 2026
    Records with strong narrative flow are frequently underdocumented. Professional wording can create the appearance of support where little documented evidence exists. Smooth prose and identifiable support are not the same thing.
