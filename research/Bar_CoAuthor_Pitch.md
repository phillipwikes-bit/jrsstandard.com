Hi Gabriela,

Thank you again for taking on the review, and for the care you are clearly bringing to it. Working through the draft myself, one thing became obvious: the piece is only half finished without a European voice, and that voice should be yours.

I would like to invite you to co-author it with me. Your name would sit on the byline as an author, alongside mine, not as a reviewer.

Here is what I have in mind for your part. The article makes a US-centered evidentiary argument: discovery, the McDonnell Douglas burden-shifting analysis, ESI obligations. Section V opens the European frame, the GDPR accountability principle and the EU AI Act's record-keeping and logging expectations for high-risk uses. I left that section deliberately as a strong skeleton, because the precise contours are yours to draw. It is the section that turns a US practitioner note into a genuinely transatlantic one, and it sits squarely in your expertise.

On placement, I think the natural home is the Oxford Business Law Blog: citable, international, and read by exactly the audience we want. If you would prefer, Lexology or JD Supra is a near-certain second option and reaches the AI-law community you are already recognized in. I am glad to defer to your judgment on venue.

A word on how I handle authorship, so there is no ambiguity: co-authorship is earned by contribution, so your name goes on the moment your section does, and nothing about the review obligates you to any of this. If the timing or the fit is not right, the review alone is already a real contribution and I am grateful for it.

Would you be open to this? If so, I will send you the working draft with Section V flagged for your expansion, and we can shape the rest together.
