We selected the better candidate.

That was the entire explanation.

No documented comparison.

No discussion of qualifications.

No reason one applicant was preferred over another.

Just one sentence.

Months later, the hiring decision was challenged.

Everyone involved believed it had been fair.

But no one could independently explain why it was made.

Not because the decision was wrong.

Because the record could not reconstruct the reasoning behind it.

This is becoming more common as organizations rely on AI to draft interview summaries, hiring recommendations, investigative reports, performance evaluations, and other workplace records.

The decision may be reasonable.

The record may look complete.

Yet months or years later, neither explains how the conclusion was reached.

I call this Decision Reconstruction Risk (DRR).

DRR exists when a record cannot answer one question:

Why?

When documentation cannot reconstruct the reasoning behind a consequential decision, meaningful review breaks down.

Bias can pass through layer after layer without being recognized.

Errors become harder to catch.

Investigators cannot tell whether policies were applied consistently.

Auditors cannot verify how the decision was reached.

Courts are left weighing conclusions instead of documented reasoning.

Most important of all, the people affected may have no real way to understand or challenge decisions that shape their employment, housing, education, benefits, or other rights.

This is not simply a documentation problem.

It is a question of accountability.

Of procedural fairness.

Of public trust.

As AI-assisted decision-making becomes a global reality, no single profession has all the answers.

Investigators see risks attorneys do not.

HR professionals ask questions auditors do not.

Civil rights advocates recognize concerns technologists may overlook.

Researchers, compliance professionals, policymakers, educators, and AI practitioners each bring perspectives that strengthen consequential decisions.

Underneath all of it is a simple idea: people deserve decisions they can understand, and a fair chance to question the ones that shape their lives.

So I would rather hear from you than talk at you.

When a decision at your organization gets a second look months later, does the record still explain why it was made?

Where does the reasoning disappear first: the comparison, the timeline, the evidence, or the rationale?

And what have you seen actually keep a record able to answer "why" long after the fact?

If you work in HR, compliance, investigations, audit, law, or AI governance, I would genuinely like to read your experience in the comments.
