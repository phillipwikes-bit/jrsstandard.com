## THE INITIATIVE (public-facing copy)

# Decision Reconstruction Risk (DRR) Initiative
### Protecting the right to know why.
*Building records that can answer "Why?"*

The Decision Reconstruction Risk (DRR) Initiative is an independent, international public-interest effort dedicated to protecting the transparency, accountability, and fairness of consequential decisions in the age of AI-assisted documentation.

Around the world, organizations increasingly rely on AI to draft investigative reports, HR records, compliance files, performance evaluations, housing determinations, case summaries, and workplace policies. AI can improve efficiency, but it also introduces an overlooked risk.

A decision may be reasonable. A record may appear complete. Yet months or years later, neither may explain how or why the conclusion was reached.

We call this failure Decision Reconstruction Risk (DRR). DRR exists when a record cannot independently explain why a consequential decision was made. When that happens, decisions become difficult to review, defend, or challenge. Unsupported assumptions can become embedded in official records. Bias may pass through multiple layers of review without detection. Errors become harder to identify and correct, because the reasoning behind a decision cannot be reconstructed from the documentation itself.

The consequences reach far beyond documentation quality. People may lose employment or be denied housing, promotions, accommodations, benefits, or educational opportunities without a record that clearly explains the basis for the decision. Investigators may be unable to determine whether policies were applied consistently. Auditors and regulators may be unable to verify compliance. Courts may be left evaluating incomplete records rather than documented reasoning.

When records cannot answer "Why?", accountability is weakened, transparency is diminished, and the practical ability of individuals to understand, question, and seek meaningful review of decisions affecting their lives is reduced.

This is not simply a documentation problem. It is a challenge to due process, procedural fairness, public trust, and the practical protection of civil and human rights.

No single profession can solve this. No single organization can solve this. No single country can solve this. Because AI-assisted decision-making is becoming a global reality, safeguarding fair and explainable decisions requires an international effort built on diverse professional expertise.

The Initiative brings together investigators, HR professionals, attorneys, judges, auditors, compliance leaders, researchers, AI practitioners, civil rights advocates, human rights organizations, public administrators, educators, policymakers, and technologists who believe that consequential decisions should remain understandable, reviewable, and accountable.

Diversity is one of the Initiative's greatest strengths. Different professions ask different questions. Different cultures recognize different risks. Different disciplines identify different forms of evidence. Different lived experiences reveal blind spots that others may never see. It is precisely this range of knowledge, experience, and professional judgment that strengthens our ability to recognize Decision Reconstruction Risk before it affects people's lives.

Whether your work protects workers, tenants, consumers, students, patients, communities, or the public, our shared principle is simple:

- People deserve decisions they can understand.
- People deserve records that can withstand independent review.
- People deserve a meaningful opportunity to question decisions that affect their rights, livelihoods, and futures.

This Initiative exists to help make that possible.

Participation is open. All resources are free to download, use, adapt, and share. We invite individuals and organizations around the world to contribute their expertise, challenge our assumptions, improve the work, and help establish stronger standards for AI-assisted documentation.

Together, we can help ensure that technology strengthens human judgment rather than obscuring it, and that the records shaping people's lives remain transparent, accountable, and worthy of public trust.

## OUR MISSION

The DRR Initiative is building an international community dedicated to protecting transparency, accountability, and human rights by improving the quality of documentation supporting consequential decisions. This work is evidence-based, interdisciplinary, and openly evaluated. It is organized around four collaborative missions.

### Mission One: Advance the understanding of Decision Reconstruction Risk
Raise awareness of DRR through research, publications, and practical examples drawn from real investigations and decision-making environments. The goal is to make DRR a recognized documentation risk across investigations, human resources, compliance, auditing, AI governance, and public administration, so it can be caught before it affects people's rights.

### Mission Two: Independently validate the Justification Review Standard (JRS)
Strong standards require independent scrutiny. The Initiative convenes an international panel of investigators, attorneys, HR professionals, auditors, researchers, AI practitioners, civil rights professionals, and compliance leaders, with reviewers on five continents, to evaluate, challenge, and strengthen JRS. This includes independent comparison research that tests whether the standard actually helps reviewers catch gaps they would otherwise miss. Validation is not meant to confirm the standard. It is meant to improve it. Constructive criticism and professional disagreement are welcomed, because they keep the framework practical, credible, and broadly applicable.

### Mission Three: Pilot JRS in real organizations
Documentation standards should be tested where decisions actually happen. The Initiative partners with organizations willing to evaluate JRS on real documentation workflows. Pilot participants help determine whether JRS improves documentation quality, whether reviewers identify more reconstructability gaps, whether records become easier to understand and defend, and where the framework should be refined. Every pilot contributes practical evidence that strengthens future versions of the standard.

### Mission Four: Equip professionals worldwide
Improving documentation requires practical, accessible tools. The Initiative provides free resources organizations can use immediately, including the Investigator Field Guide, the Reviewer Reference Guide, the Reviewer Training Program with a certificate of completion, JRS worksheets and checklists, and future research publications. The goal is to remove barriers so organizations of every size can improve documentation quality and better protect the people their decisions affect.

## WHY DIVERSITY MATTERS

The most consequential decisions affect people from every community, profession, and culture, so improving those decisions requires equally diverse perspectives.

- Investigators identify evidentiary gaps.
- Attorneys recognize legal vulnerabilities.
- HR professionals understand organizational processes.
- Auditors detect control failures.
- Civil rights advocates identify disparate impacts.
- Human rights practitioners recognize procedural injustices.
- AI practitioners understand system limitations.
- Researchers evaluate evidence objectively.

No single profession sees every risk. Together, they do. The Initiative therefore seeks participation across disciplines, sectors, and countries, because stronger documentation emerges from diverse expertise rather than uniform thinking.

## Editor's notes (what changed, and the honesty guardrails)

1. **Reframed from "help validate my framework" to "protect people's right to understand and challenge decisions."** The rights stakes (employment, housing, benefits, due process, procedural fairness) now lead, which is a mission investigators, lawyers, auditors, and civil and human rights advocates can all join. JRS and the studies become the method, not the point.

2. **Motto added.** "Protecting the right to know why" is the human-rights headline; "Building records that can answer 'Why?'" drops to a subtitle. Alternates are listed up top.

3. **De-personalized per your strategic recommendation.** The body describes an independent, community-supported initiative; your role appears once, separately, in the founding line. The bio matches how you are described on the live site (former Lead Civil Rights Officer, Maryland Commission on Civil Rights).

4. **The four missions map to real assets from the master tracker:** the international validation panel (Mission Two), the independent comparison study (folded into Mission Two, described generically), the organizational pilots (Mission Three), and the training program plus the three investigator guide editions (Mission Four). Links /guide and /reviewer are verified live.

5. **Claims kept defensible.** I wrote "reviewers on five continents" rather than a specific count, because the tracker directs using the completer sample for public claims, and five continents holds for completers. I did not state reviewer totals, pilot counts, or reliability statistics in the public copy, so nothing there can be read as inflated. If you want to cite hard numbers, the defensible completer figures (8 countries, 5 continents) and the pre-registered reliability results (Gwet's AC1 0.74 experts, 0.63 reviewers) are available to add with that context.

6. **Blind-study guardrail respected.** The Arm B comparison study is described only in general terms ("independent comparison research"). The public copy does not reveal the method, the five conditions, or that reviewers are assigned to arms, so publishing this does not compromise the blind.

7. **No long dashes and no banned prose patterns.** Removed the em dashes and the divider characters from your draft; the single hyphen in "AI-assisted" is fine.

Sitting judges usually cannot join advocacy initiatives without raising impartiality concerns, so I kept the broader list but you may want to drop "judges" or soften it to "former judges and judicial educators" depending on who you are actually recruiting.
