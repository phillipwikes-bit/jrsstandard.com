To the Editors, *AI and Ethics*:

I am submitting a Perspective article for your consideration, titled "When the Record Cannot Stand on Business: Decision Reconstruction Risk as a Documentation-Governance Gap in AI-Assisted Records."

The article introduces and names a governance problem that the current AI-governance literature, which is largely model-centric, does not directly measure: as generative AI drafts more consequential records, fluent output can read as complete while lacking the evidentiary basis that would let an independent reviewer reconstruct and defend the decision. I call this property Decision Reconstruction Risk (DRR). The article argues that DRR is a distinct, output-level, workflow-independent property, proposes a record-level review standard as a control, and sets out a pre-registered agenda for testing whether the control adds value over unaided expert judgment.

I believe it fits *AI and Ethics* for three reasons. First, it addresses accountability and responsible deployment in practice, drawn from real accountability work in civil rights documentation, rather than from theory alone. Second, it reframes a fast-moving technical development as a governance and ethics question about who a record must be able to answer to. Third, as a Perspective it advances a concept and a testable agenda; the empirical validation is described transparently as pre-registered and in progress, and no empirical result is claimed here. A companion empirical paper will report results when the pre-registered sample is complete.

This work is original, is not under consideration elsewhere, and has not been published previously. JRS is a standard I created and is in a validation phase; the article makes no proven-effectiveness claim and I state this competing interest openly. I am the sole author. Contributors to the associated validation study are acknowledged, not listed as authors.

Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
Phillip Wikes
