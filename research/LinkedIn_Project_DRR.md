## 2. Full narrative version (for the website / About section, no character limit)

The Decision Reconstruction Risk (DRR) Initiative is an independent public-interest effort to improve the quality, transparency, and defensibility of consequential decisions in the age of AI-assisted documentation.

As organizations increasingly rely on AI to draft investigative reports, HR records, compliance documentation, policies, and workplace decisions, an overlooked risk is emerging.

A decision may be reasonable. A record may appear complete. Yet months later, neither may explain how the conclusion was actually reached.

We refer to this documentation failure as Decision Reconstruction Risk (DRR). DRR exists when a record cannot independently explain why a consequential decision was made. When that happens, reviewers, investigators, auditors, attorneys, regulators, and courts may be unable to reconstruct the decision-making process from the record itself.

The DRR Initiative brings together investigators, HR professionals, attorneys, auditors, compliance leaders, researchers, AI practitioners, and public administrators to improve documentation before these failures occur. Our objectives are to:

- Define and advance understanding of Decision Reconstruction Risk.
- Promote documentation practices that improve independent review and defensibility.
- Develop practical resources for investigators and reviewers.
- Encourage responsible use of AI-assisted documentation.
- Build an international community committed to fair, transparent, and reconstructable decision records.

Participation is open. Resources are free. Organizations are encouraged to download, use, adapt, and share the materials throughout their teams.

Together we can help ensure that important decisions remain understandable, reviewable, and defensible long after they are made.

**Mission:** To reduce Decision Reconstruction Risk by improving the quality, traceability, and defensibility of records supporting consequential decisions.

**Vision:** A world in which every consequential decision can be independently reconstructed, understood, and fairly reviewed from the record itself.

**Core principles:**
- Transparency over assumption.
- Evidence before conclusion.
- Human accountability for consequential decisions.
- AI as an assistant, not a substitute for documented reasoning.
- Documentation that withstands independent review.

**Current public resources:**
- Investigator Field Guide (free download): jrsstandard.com/guide
- Reviewer Training Program: jrsstandard.com/reviewer
- Reviewer Reference Guide
- Decision Reconstruction Risk briefing papers
- JRS Justification Review Standard

**Join the initiative.** If your work involves investigations, workplace decisions, compliance, civil rights, HR, legal review, AI governance, auditing, or public administration, we invite you to participate. You can contribute by:
- Downloading and using the free resources.
- Sharing them with colleagues.
- Providing feedback.
- Participating in future validation studies.
- Helping identify new examples of Decision Reconstruction Risk.
- Promoting stronger documentation practices within your organization.

## Editor's notes (what changed and why)

1. **Pulled the "Why this positioning is powerful" paragraph out of the public copy.** It compares DRR to "technical debt" and "confirmation bias" and describes the goal of making DRR "the accepted term" for a failure mode. That is sound internal strategy, but on a public LinkedIn Project it reads as marketing-about-the-marketing and undercuts the credibility of a public-interest framing. Keep it as an internal north-star note, not published text.

2. **Trimmed the description to fit LinkedIn's 2,000-character Project-description limit.** The full draft runs well over. Section 1 is the version that fits; Section 2 is the full narrative for your website or profile About section, where there is no limit.

3. **Removed the divider characters** (the long horizontal rules). LinkedIn Project descriptions render as plain text, so those would show up as literal symbols. Line breaks and bullet dots do the separation instead.

4. **No long dashes and no banned prose patterns** in any of the copy, per the house style.

5. **Verified, not assumed:** /guide and /reviewer both resolve (vercel.json), the contact email matches the SSOT, and the bio line matches investigator-guides.html. The podcast link and 23:45 timestamp are yours as given; I did not alter them.

### One judgment call for you
The message says "I am leading an international effort." That is fair given the international reviewer panel and study, but if you would rather stay strictly modest, "I am running an independent international effort" reads a touch less self-appointed. Your call; I left your original phrasing in place.
