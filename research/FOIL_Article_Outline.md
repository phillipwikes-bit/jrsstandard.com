# Co-Authored Research Article — Outline (Working Draft)

**Working title:** Documentation as a Governance Layer: Does a Structured Review Standard Identify Characteristics Associated with Defensible Public-Records Determinations?

## 0. Abstract (to be written after Results)
One paragraph: the problem (records that read as complete but cannot be reconstructed on appeal), the standard (JRS), the design (public determinations paired with documented outcomes, JRS read recorded blind to outcome), the result (to be filled at n ≥ 20), and the contribution (documentation quality as an independent governance metric complementary to FOIA technology modernization).

## 1. Introduction and Background
- 1.1 The defensibility gap in public-records determinations. A FOIL/records determination can be facially complete yet fail on appeal or in Article 78 litigation because its basis is not reconstructable from the record itself.
- 1.2 Why now: the federal modernization moment. On **May 28, 2026**, the Chief FOIA Officers Council announced a government-wide initiative to identify and improve FOIA technologies, document-review tools, and related applications. As agencies evaluate new technology, an equally important question is whether the resulting documentation remains independently reviewable, evidentiary, and defensible.
- 1.3 The measurement gap. Technology inventories (e.g., the FOIA-technology inventory described by Dr. Jeremy Frazier) measure *what software agencies use*. Very few measure *whether the resulting records are actually reviewable*. JRS addresses that gap.
- 1.4 Positioning. JRS does not evaluate FOIA software. It evaluates whether the documentation produced through any workflow, manual or AI-assisted, remains sufficiently complete for an independent reviewer to reconstruct and assess the agency's reasoning. This is a governance layer that sits above any particular technology — complementary to case-management systems, eDiscovery platforms, and AI document-review tools, not competitive with them.

## 2. Purpose and Research Questions (expanded)
- Primary: This pilot evaluates whether a structured documentation-review standard identifies characteristics associated with successful public-records determinations.
- Secondary: The project also explores whether documentation quality can serve as an independent governance metric alongside existing FOIA technology-modernization efforts.
- Research questions:
  - RQ1. Is a record's JRS read (Ready / Needs work / Gap), recorded before the outcome is known, associated with the documented appeal outcome?
  - RQ2. Can the JRS conditions be applied consistently to real public-records determinations across cases and reviewers?
  - RQ3. What documentation characteristics distinguish determinations that hold up on review from those that do not?

## 3. The Justification Review Standard (brief)
- The single question: can a later, independent reviewer reconstruct how a conclusion was reached from the record alone?
- The five conditions (RC1 Reconstructability, RC2 Basis Identification, RC3 Chronology, RC4 Decision-Process Traceability, RC5 Evidentiary Sufficiency) and the three-level read (Ready / Needs work / Gap).
- Named risk: Decision Reconstruction Risk (DRR).

## 4. Methods
- 4.1 Design. Retrospective, records-based. For each case, a real public determination (FOIL appeal decision, NY Committee on Open Government opinion, or Article 78 ruling) where the record's reasoning was at issue is paired with its documented outcome. The JRS read is recorded first, from the record itself, blind to the outcome; the documented outcome and public citation are then recorded.
- 4.2 Materials. Public material only; de-identified; each case carries a public citation/source. No internal or confidential agency material.
- 4.3 Sample. Target 20–30 cases with a deliberate spread of outcomes, collected in small batches. **Current verified sample: n = 7** (7 distinct public sources; collected 2026-06-26 to 2026-07-06).
- 4.4 Outcome coding. Documented outcomes are coded from the cited decision (e.g., determination upheld vs. overturned / disclosure ordered). **Coding scheme to be finalized and each case's outcome verified against the cited decision before analysis** (the raw store currently uses `challenged` / `failed_appeal` labels that will be mapped to a published, auditable upheld/overturned scheme).
- 4.5 Reliability. Where a second reviewer scores a subset, inter-rater agreement on the JRS read is reported (Gwet's AC1 primary, consistent with the broader JRS validation program).
- 4.6 Analysis plan. Association between JRS read and outcome (exact test appropriate to small cell counts), reported with effect size and interval; descriptive characterization of the documentation features that recur in overturned determinations. Pre-registered thresholds govern any inferential claim.

## 5. Results (PLACEHOLDER — data collection in progress)
- **Do not populate with inferential claims until n ≥ 20 with a spread of outcomes.**
- Current descriptive status only (n = 7, not for inference): JRS reads to date are 5 "Ready" and 2 "Needs work / review", 0 "Gap"; outcomes span two documented categories. The absence of "Gap" reads and the small n mean no association is claimed at this stage; the spread requirement is not yet met.
- To be completed: cross-tabulation of JRS read × outcome; agreement statistics; the documentation-characteristic analysis.

## 6. Discussion
- 6.1 Documentation quality as an independent governance metric, measurable regardless of the software or AI system used to produce the record.
- 6.2 Complementarity with FOIA technology modernization. JRS measures the reviewability of outputs that technology inventories do not capture; it can sit alongside a technology inventory as an outcome-quality layer.
- 6.3 Practical implications for records officers: writing determinations that reconstruct their own basis reduces reversals and supports consistency across officers.

## 7. Potential Contribution to Federal FOIA Modernization
Data collected during this pilot may inform future discussions regarding:
- Documentation quality metrics
- AI-assisted records-review governance
- Explainability of agency determinations
- Reviewer consistency
- Documentation risk indicators
- Training and quality assurance for FOIA professionals

## 8. Limitations
- Small, growing sample (n = 7 at outline stage); single-domain (public records / FOIL); cases are author/advisor-selected from published decisions; outcome coding depends on the cited record; generalization beyond the sampled jurisdictions is not claimed. JRS remains in a validation phase and makes no proven-effectiveness claims.

## 9. Conclusion
- The drafting tool and the technology stack change; the evidentiary test does not. A structured documentation-review standard offers an outcome-quality metric that is complementary to FOIA technology modernization and applicable regardless of the workflow used.
